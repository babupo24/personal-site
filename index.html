About Me

I am a PhD-level experimental and computational physicist specializing in Monte Carlo simulations, charged-particle transport, detector modeling, and high-energy nuclear/particle physics. My work spans GEANT-based detector simulation, signal digitization, ROOT/PyROOT data analysis, and large-scale scientific computing. I have contributed extensively to the STAR and ePIC collaborations, with hands-on expertise in detector operation, data acquisition, and instrument modeling.

I am also experienced in Python, C++, MATLAB, TensorFlow/PyTorch, and numerical methods for scientific computing. My background includes thin-film fabrication, material characterization, and device prototyping during my early graduate research. I hold a U.S. Permanent Resident (Green Card) and am open to relocation.

Professional Experience

Postdoctoral Researcher — Temple University (Nov 2023 – Aug 2024)

Developed C++-based digitization algorithms for MPGD detectors, modeling signal formation for the ePIC experiment. Simulated charged-particle interactions in detector gas/materials using Monte Carlo and FEM methods. Contributed to detector design through physics-based modeling and reproducible, version-controlled software pipelines.

Research Assistant — STAR Collaboration, Temple University (Aug 2019 – May 2023)

Designed and ran GEANT and PYTHIA simulations to model detector response in proton–proton collisions at √s = 200 GeV. Performed high-energy data analysis of pions, muons, and protons, including azimuthal correlations and cross-section studies. Operated beamline detectors and electronics during live RHIC runs, ensuring calibration and real-time monitoring.

Research Assistant (M.Sc.) — Kathmandu University (2012 – 2014)

Engineered and characterized ZnO thin films and dye-sensitized solar cells (DSSC). Performed STM, XRD, and optical/electrical analysis to study thin-film behavior under varying fabrication conditions.
